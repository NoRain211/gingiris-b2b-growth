# B2B SaaS Growth Anti-Patterns

Things that feel productive but kill your company. The stuff consultants charge you to learn after you've already made the mistake.

---

## Premature Scaling

You don't have a growth problem. You have a "nobody wants this yet" problem, and you're throwing money at distribution to avoid facing it.

### Hiring SDRs before you have PMF

**What it looks like:** You've got 3 SDRs booking meetings, a 2% close rate, and a CEO who says "we just need more pipeline."

**Why it happens:** Founders confuse revenue with product-market fit. You closed a few deals through your network, assumed the playbook was repeatable, and hired people to run a playbook that doesn't exist yet.

**How to fix it:** Founders should be selling until close rates stabilize above 15-20% and you can articulate exactly who buys, why, and how long it takes. If you can't write down your sales process in one page, you don't have one.

**Ask yourself:** "If I handed our sales process doc to a stranger, could they close a deal in 60 days?"

### Spending on paid acquisition before organic converts

**What it looks like:** You're spending $15K/month on Google Ads, your CAC is 3x your ACV, and you're telling the board "we're investing in growth."

**Why it happens:** Paid feels controllable. You turn the dial, leads come in. Organic takes months. Impatient founders and boards want numbers now.

**How to fix it:** Get 50 customers organically first. If you can't, paid won't save you — it'll just burn cash faster. Paid acquisition amplifies what already works. It doesn't create demand that doesn't exist.

**Ask yourself:** "Are any customers finding us without us paying for their attention?"

### Building a partner program before you have customers

**What it looks like:** You have a "Partner Portal," a co-branded PDF, and zero partner-sourced revenue.

**Why it happens:** Partnerships feel like leverage. "If we just get HubSpot to refer us..." It's easier to fantasize about someone else selling for you than to sell yourself.

**How to fix it:** Get to 100 customers first. Then look at which tools they already use, where they hang out, and who they trust. Partner with those people. Partnerships work when you bring something to the table. At 5 customers, you bring nothing.

**Ask yourself:** "What would our partner actually gain from referring us right now?"

### The "we need more top-of-funnel" fallacy

**What it looks like:** 1,000 signups/month, 50 activate, 5 convert. CEO says "we need 10,000 signups/month." No, you need to fix your 5% activation rate.

**Why it happens:** Top-of-funnel is the most visible metric. It's also the easiest to move. Activation and retention are harder, scarier, and require admitting the product might have problems.

**How to fix it:** Work the funnel bottom-up. Fix retention first (are people getting value?), then activation (are people reaching the value?), then acquisition (are the right people showing up?). Pouring water into a leaky bucket is not a growth strategy.

**Ask yourself:** "If we 10x'd signups tomorrow with zero other changes, how much revenue would that actually add?"

### Real examples of companies that scaled too early

- **Homejoy** raised $38M, scaled to 30+ cities before solving cleaner retention. Shut down in 2015.
- **Beepi** (used car marketplace) raised $150M, expanded to multiple states before the unit economics worked. Dead.
- **Exec** (cleaning service) scaled to multiple cities, couldn't maintain quality. Acqui-hired for parts.

The pattern is always the same: raise money, mistake capital for validation, scale a broken model, run out of runway.

## Vanity Metrics Traps

If a metric only goes up and never makes you uncomfortable, it's not telling you anything useful.

### Total registered users

**What it looks like:** Your investor deck says "50,000 users!" Your Stripe dashboard says $4,200 MRR.

**Why it happens:** Total users is the ultimate feel-good number. It only goes up. It includes everyone who signed up once, poked around for 30 seconds, and never came back. You report it because it's the biggest number you have.

**How to fix it:** Track weekly active users (WAU) or monthly active users (MAU), and be honest about the definition of "active." Logging in doesn't count. Performing a core value action counts.

**Ask yourself:** "How many of these 'users' did something meaningful in the last 30 days?"

### Page views without conversion tracking

**What it looks like:** "Our blog got 100K views last month!" How many signed up? "We don't track that."

**Why it happens:** Traffic is easy to measure and sounds impressive. Attribution is hard. Nobody set up UTM parameters or conversion tracking because the marketing team was too busy writing the next blog post.

**How to fix it:** Every page needs a clear next action. Track the action, not the view. If your blog post has no CTA and no conversion tracking, it's a hobby, not a marketing channel.

**Ask yourself:** "Can I trace a single customer back to a specific piece of content?"

### MQLs without pipeline attribution

**What it looks like:** Marketing reports 500 MQLs this quarter. Sales says pipeline is dry. Everyone's confused and pointing fingers.

**Why it happens:** Marketing is incentivized to generate MQLs, so the MQL definition gets looser over time. Downloaded a whitepaper? MQL. Visited the pricing page? MQL. Eventually "MQL" means "has a pulse and an email address."

**How to fix it:** Align marketing and sales on a single pipeline metric. Track MQL-to-SQL conversion rate, SQL-to-opportunity rate, and opportunity-to-close rate. If your MQL-to-close rate is below 1%, your MQL definition is broken.

**Ask yourself:** "What percentage of our MQLs would sales actually want to talk to?"

### "We have 10K signups!" but 200 active users

**What it looks like:** You celebrate every signup milestone on Slack. Meanwhile, the product is a ghost town.

**Why it happens:** Signups are the dopamine hit. Activation is where the work is. Fixing onboarding, reducing time-to-value, reaching out to churned users — none of that feels as exciting as watching the signup counter tick up.

**How to fix it:** Set your signup-to-active ratio as a core metric. If it's below 20%, stop all acquisition spending and fix onboarding. A 2% activation rate means 98% of your marketing spend is wasted.

**Ask yourself:** "If we paused all marketing today, how many of our existing signups would still become paying customers?"

### Social media followers as a growth metric

**What it looks like:** "We hit 15K followers on LinkedIn!" Your pipeline hasn't moved in two months.

**Why it happens:** Social media gives instant feedback — likes, comments, follows. Revenue doesn't. So you optimize for the thing that gives you a dopamine hit.

**How to fix it:** Social is awareness, not acquisition. Track how many website visits, signups, or demo requests come from social. If the answer is "we don't know," social isn't a growth channel — it's a content hobby.

**Ask yourself:** "Can I name three customers who found us through social media?"

### How to identify vanity metrics in your own dashboard

A metric is vanity if:
- It only ever goes up
- It can't be tied to revenue within two steps
- Nobody would change their behavior based on it going down 20%
- You report it to feel good, not to make decisions
- It requires no context to sound impressive

Go through every metric on your dashboard and ask: "If this dropped 30% next month, what would we do differently?" If the answer is "nothing" or "I don't know," kill it.

## Pricing Mistakes

Pricing is the single highest-leverage thing you can change and the one most founders avoid touching because it feels risky. It's not. Keeping bad pricing is risky.

### Pricing too low and attracting the wrong customers

**What it looks like:** Your $29/month plan attracts solopreneurs who submit 40 support tickets and churn in 2 months. Your ideal customer would happily pay $200/month but perceives you as a toy because of the price.

**Why it happens:** Fear. You think nobody will pay more. You're wrong. You're also anchoring to competitors who are also pricing too low.

**How to fix it:** Raise prices for new customers by 50%. Track what happens. In most cases, conversion rate barely moves, revenue per customer goes up, and customer quality improves. Patrick Campbell's data shows the majority of SaaS companies are underpriced by 2-3x.

**Ask yourself:** "Would our best customers still buy if we doubled the price?"

### Not raising prices for 2+ years

**What it looks like:** Your product has 10x more features than it did at launch. Your prices haven't changed. Your costs have.

**Why it happens:** Inertia. Fear of backlash from existing customers. The belief that grandfathered pricing builds loyalty (it doesn't — it builds entitlement).

**How to fix it:** Raise prices annually. Grandfather existing customers for 6-12 months, then migrate them. Communicate the value they've received since their last pricing update. Most customers won't blink. The ones who threaten to leave over a 20% increase were going to churn anyway.

**Ask yourself:** "When was the last time we raised prices, and what has changed in the product since then?"

### Per-seat pricing when your value isn't per-seat

**What it looks like:** You charge per seat for an analytics tool. Customers add one admin and share the login. Usage is high, revenue is low.

**Why it happens:** Per-seat is the default because Salesforce does it. But per-seat only works when each seat gets independent value (CRM, email, design tools). If the value is in the data or the output, per-seat incentivizes fewer seats.

**How to fix it:** Price on the value axis. If you're a data platform, price on data volume or queries. If you're a marketing tool, price on contacts or sends. The pricing axis should align with the thing that grows as the customer gets more value.

**Ask yourself:** "Does adding another seat mean the customer is getting more value, or just sharing access?"

### Free tier that's too generous

**What it looks like:** Your free plan has 90% of your paid features. Conversion to paid is 1.5%. You have a "freemium problem."

**Why it happens:** You wanted to remove all friction from adoption. You succeeded — you also removed all reasons to pay. The free plan is so good that upgrading feels like a nice-to-have, not a need.

**How to fix it:** Free should let users experience core value but hit a natural wall. The wall should be usage-based (not feature-based when possible). When they hit the wall, they should already understand the value well enough that paying is obvious. Audit your free plan: if a user can run their business on it, your free plan is your product.

**Ask yourself:** "What's the specific moment a free user realizes they need to upgrade? If that moment doesn't exist, you don't have a freemium model — you have a free product."

### Enterprise pricing without enterprise features

**What it looks like:** Your "Enterprise" plan is $500/month and includes... the same product with an SSO integration you haven't built yet and a "dedicated account manager" who's actually your only CS rep.

**Why it happens:** You see enterprise revenue as the promised land and slap an enterprise label on your product before it's ready. Enterprise buyers have checklists — SOC 2, SSO, SAML, audit logs, SLAs, custom contracts — and you have none of them.

**How to fix it:** Don't sell enterprise until you can deliver enterprise. Build the compliance and security features first. Get SOC 2. Build SSO. Add audit logs. Then price it at 3-5x your highest self-serve plan. Enterprise isn't a price tier — it's a product tier.

**Ask yourself:** "If a procurement team sent us a security questionnaire today, could we fill it out?"

### Discounting to close deals and destroying your pricing integrity

**What it looks like:** Every deal gets 20-30% off because "that's just how sales works." Your average selling price is 40% below list price. New prospects expect a discount before you even quote.

**Why it happens:** Sales reps are incentivized to close, not to protect pricing. The first discount sets a precedent. Word gets around ("just ask for 25% off, they always say yes"). Now your list price is fiction.

**How to fix it:** Hold the line. Offer value instead of discounts — extra implementation support, extended trials, additional seats. If you must discount, cap it at 10% for annual prepayment only. Train sales to say "this is the price" and mean it.

**Ask yourself:** "What percentage of our deals close at list price? If it's below 50%, we don't have a pricing problem — we have a sales culture problem."

## Wrong Growth Model

PLG and SLG aren't ideologies. They're distribution strategies. Using the wrong one is like putting a jet engine on a bicycle.

### Trying PLG when your product requires implementation

**What it looks like:** You've built a self-serve signup flow for a data integration platform that requires mapping 15 custom fields, connecting to an internal database, and a 3-week implementation. Your time-to-value is 4 weeks. Your trial is 14 days.

**Why it happens:** PLG is trendy. Every VC asks "is it PLG?" You want to say yes. But PLG requires the user to get to an "aha moment" fast — minutes to hours, not weeks.

**How to fix it:** If time-to-value exceeds a few days, you need human involvement. That doesn't mean heavy enterprise sales — it can be a lightweight demo + guided onboarding. Hybrid models work. Pretending your complex product is self-serve doesn't.

**Ask yourself:** "Can a new user get meaningful value from our product in under an hour with no help?"

### Trying SLG when your ACV is $20/month

**What it looks like:** You have a sales team doing hour-long demos for a $240/year product. Your CAC is $800. Your LTV:CAC ratio is 0.6. You're losing money on every customer and trying to make it up on volume.

**Why it happens:** The founders came from enterprise sales backgrounds and defaulted to what they know. Or the product started as a consulting offering and got productized without rethinking distribution.

**How to fix it:** At sub-$5K ACV, you need self-serve or at minimum low-touch sales (automated emails, in-app prompts, one-click purchase). If you can't sell without a human in the loop at this price point, either automate the sales process or raise prices until a human is justified.

**Ask yourself:** "How many deals does a rep need to close per month to justify their fully-loaded cost?"

### Building a self-serve funnel for a product that needs a demo

**What it looks like:** You've spent 6 months on a perfect self-serve onboarding flow. Signup is beautiful. Activation is 3% because users don't understand what they're looking at.

**Why it happens:** You assumed your product was intuitive because you built it. It's not. Some products genuinely need a human to explain how they fit into an existing workflow. That's not a failure — it's a product characteristic.

**How to fix it:** Add a "Book a demo" CTA that's as prominent as your "Sign up free" button. Route high-intent users (visited pricing, viewed features page 3x) to sales. Let self-serve run for low-intent traffic, but don't bet the company on it.

**Ask yourself:** "What percentage of self-serve signups reach the core value moment without any human help?"

### Signs you're using the wrong growth model

- **Wrong PLG signals:** Time-to-value > 1 week, activation rate < 10%, most paying customers came through sales anyway, users need training to understand the product.
- **Wrong SLG signals:** ACV < $5K, sales cycle > 3 months for a simple product, customers say "I just want to buy it online," competitors are PLG and winning.
- **The honest test:** Look at your last 20 customers. How did they actually buy? Not how you wanted them to buy — how they actually bought. That's your growth model. Build for reality.

## Community Theater

"Community-led growth" has become the thing people say when they don't have a distribution strategy. Most B2B "communities" are ghost towns with a content calendar.

### Building a Discord with 5,000 members and 3 active ones

**What it looks like:** You launched a Discord, invited everyone on your email list, posted a welcome message, and... silence. Occasionally someone asks a support question. You call this "our community."

**Why it happens:** Communities are easy to create and hard to build. The launch is the fun part. The daily engagement, moderation, and value creation is the hard part that nobody plans for.

**How to fix it:** A community needs three things: a shared identity, regular value delivery, and peer-to-peer interaction (not just brand-to-member). If your "community" is just you posting announcements, it's a broadcast channel. Start small — 20 engaged members beats 5,000 lurkers.

**Ask yourself:** "If we stopped posting for a month, would anyone notice?"

### "Community-led growth" that's actually a support channel

**What it looks like:** 80% of posts in your "community" are "How do I do X?" and "Is anyone else getting this error?" Your community manager is actually your first support tier.

**Why it happens:** Users go where they can get answers fastest. If your community is the fastest path to support, that's what it becomes. You didn't build a community — you built a free support channel and gave it a friendlier name.

**How to fix it:** Separate support from community. Have a proper support channel (help desk, docs, in-app chat). The community should be about use cases, best practices, and connecting peers. If all conversations are about your product's bugs, that's a product problem, not a community opportunity.

**Ask yourself:** "If we removed all support-related posts, what would be left?"

### Ambassador programs where nobody does anything

**What it looks like:** You have 50 "ambassadors" with a Notion page of perks. Zero of them have referred a customer. They joined for the free swag.

**Why it happens:** Ambassador programs assume your users are so passionate they'll sell for you in exchange for a t-shirt. Most B2B products don't inspire that kind of devotion. People have jobs.

**How to fix it:** Don't build an ambassador program until you have organic champions — people who are already recommending you without being asked. Then give those specific people tools and incentives. The program should formalize behavior that already exists, not try to create behavior from scratch.

**Ask yourself:** "How many customers have referred someone to us without any incentive in the last 6 months?"

### The difference between a community and an audience

An **audience** consumes your content. A **community** interacts with each other. If all interactions go through you (brand → member), you have an audience. If members help each other, share experiences, and create value independently, you have a community.

Most B2B companies have an audience and call it a community. That's fine — audiences are valuable. But don't expect community-like engagement from an audience, and don't staff a "community team" to manage a newsletter.

## Content Marketing Pitfalls

Content marketing works. Most content marketing doesn't. The gap is almost always the same set of mistakes.

### Blog posts nobody searches for

**What it looks like:** You publish "Our Journey to Product-Market Fit" and "Why We Chose Rust for Our Backend." 47 views, 45 of which are your team.

**Why it happens:** You're writing about what you find interesting, not what your customers are searching for. Your blog is a journal, not a marketing channel.

**How to fix it:** Start with keyword research. Find what your ICP is actually searching for, validate there's volume, and write the best answer on the internet. Then supplement with opinion pieces — but only after you've earned traffic with search-driven content.

**Ask yourself:** "Is anyone Googling the title of this blog post?"

### "Thought leadership" that's just opinion pieces

**What it looks like:** Your CEO publishes hot takes on the future of the industry every week. They get 12 likes on LinkedIn (8 from employees). No pipeline impact.

**Why it happens:** Thought leadership sounds prestigious. It's also the easiest content to produce — just have opinions. Real thought leadership requires original research, data, or frameworks that others adopt. Hot takes don't count.

**How to fix it:** Thought leadership should be anchored in data or unique experience. Publish original research, benchmarks, or frameworks. "Here's what we think about AI" is an opinion. "We analyzed 10,000 sales calls and here's what the top 1% of reps do differently" is thought leadership.

**Ask yourself:** "Does this piece contain anything our competitors couldn't also write?"

### SEO content that ranks but doesn't convert

**What it looks like:** Your blog gets 200K monthly visits. 50 signups per month. You're ranking for informational keywords with zero purchase intent.

**Why it happens:** You optimized for volume, not intent. "What is CRM" gets 50K monthly searches but nobody reading it is ready to buy your CRM. You confused traffic with demand.

**How to fix it:** Map content to funnel stages. Top-of-funnel content builds awareness, but you need middle and bottom-of-funnel content that captures purchase intent. "Best CRM for startups" has 1/10th the volume but 100x the conversion rate. Balance your content calendar accordingly.

**Ask yourself:** "Of our top 20 ranking pages, how many target keywords with purchase intent?"

### Writing for other marketers instead of your ICP

**What it looks like:** Your most popular posts are "How We Grew to $1M ARR" and "Our Tech Stack for Content Marketing." Your product is a supply chain management tool.

**Why it happens:** Marketers write about marketing because it's what they know and what gets engagement in their professional circles. Your Twitter followers are other marketers, not supply chain managers.

**How to fix it:** Interview 10 customers. Ask what they Google when they have a problem your product solves. Write about that. Your content should be uninteresting to marketers and invaluable to your ICP.

**Ask yourself:** "Would our ideal customer find this post in their normal workflow, or only if they followed our company blog?"

### The content quantity vs. quality trap

**What it looks like:** You publish 4 blog posts per week. All are 800-word summaries of obvious advice. None rank. None get shared. Nobody remembers them.

**Why it happens:** Someone said "consistency is key" and the team interpreted that as "publish constantly regardless of quality." The content calendar became a production schedule, not a strategy.

**How to fix it:** One exceptional piece per month beats four forgettable pieces per week. Put all the time you'd spend on 16 mediocre posts into 1-2 that are genuinely the best resource on the internet for that topic. Depth and originality compound. Volume without quality doesn't.

**Ask yourself:** "Would I bookmark this post if a competitor published it?"

## Sales Process Failures

Bad sales processes don't just lose deals — they lose the right deals and close the wrong ones. Then you spend the next year supporting customers who never should have bought.

### Demoing features instead of solving problems

**What it looks like:** Your demo is a 45-minute product walkthrough. "And here you can see we have filters... and here's the reporting dashboard... and over here is the integration panel..." The prospect checks their phone at minute 12.

**Why it happens:** Product demos are comfortable. You're in control, showing the thing you know best. Discovery is uncomfortable — you have to ask hard questions and risk hearing "we don't actually need this."

**How to fix it:** Structure demos around the prospect's specific problems. "You mentioned your team spends 4 hours/week on manual data entry — let me show you exactly how that goes to zero." Every feature you show should connect directly to a pain point they articulated. If you can't make that connection, don't show it.

**Ask yourself:** "Could a prospect describe to their boss what our product does for them after watching our demo?"

### No discovery call — going straight to demo

**What it looks like:** Prospect books a demo. You open Zoom, share your screen, and start clicking. 30 minutes later you ask "any questions?" They say "looks great, we'll get back to you." They never do.

**Why it happens:** Reps want to show the product because it feels like progress. Asking questions feels slow. There's also a fear that if you delay the demo, the prospect will lose interest.

**How to fix it:** Separate discovery from demo. Spend 20-30 minutes understanding their current process, pain points, timeline, budget, and decision-making process. Then schedule a tailored demo that addresses what you learned. This takes more time but dramatically increases close rates because you're showing them their solution, not your product.

**Ask yourself:** "Before our last demo, did we know the prospect's top 3 pain points, budget range, and decision process?"

### Single-threaded deals

**What it looks like:** You've been talking to Sarah for 3 months. Sarah loves the product. Sarah's boss hasn't heard of you. Sarah leaves the company. Deal is dead.

**Why it happens:** Talking to one champion is comfortable. They're responsive, positive, and engaged. Asking to meet their boss or other stakeholders feels pushy. So you don't.

**How to fix it:** Every deal should have at least 3 contacts at the prospect company: a champion, an economic buyer, and an end user. If your champion won't introduce you to others, they're either not actually a champion or the deal isn't real. Multi-threading isn't optional for deals over $10K.

**Ask yourself:** "If our main contact left the company tomorrow, would the deal survive?"

### Not qualifying out bad-fit prospects

**What it looks like:** Your pipeline is full of prospects who "might" buy. Sales cycle is 9 months. Win rate is 8%. Your CRM is a graveyard of zombie deals.

**Why it happens:** Reps don't want to shrink their pipeline. Managers don't want to see pipeline value drop. So everyone pretends deals are alive long after they're dead. Every prospect stays in the funnel because "you never know."

**How to fix it:** Define your disqualification criteria as clearly as your qualification criteria. If a prospect doesn't have the budget, the problem, the authority, or the timeline — remove them. A clean pipeline with 20 qualified deals beats a bloated pipeline with 200 maybes. Track disqualification rate — if it's near zero, your reps aren't qualifying.

**Ask yourself:** "How many deals in our pipeline right now have had zero activity in 30+ days?"

### Discounting on the first ask

**What it looks like:** Prospect says "that's a bit more than we expected." Rep immediately offers 15% off. Prospect says "can you do 25%?" Rep says yes. You just set the floor for every future negotiation with this account.

**Why it happens:** Reps fear losing the deal. There's often no pricing playbook or negotiation training. The path of least resistance is to give the discount and book the deal.

**How to fix it:** Train reps to respond to price pushback with questions, not discounts. "What budget did you have in mind?" "What's the cost of not solving this problem?" "What would make this a no-brainer at this price?" If you must negotiate, trade value — longer contract, case study agreement, referral commitment — never just drop the price.

**Ask yourself:** "When was the last time a rep said 'no' to a discount request and still closed the deal?"

### 6-month sales cycles for a $5K deal

**What it looks like:** Your average deal size is $5K/year. Your average sales cycle is 5 months. Your CAC exceeds your first-year revenue. You need 2+ years of retention just to break even on acquisition.

**Why it happens:** Either your product is being sold to the wrong buyer (too senior, requiring too many approvals), your sales process has too many steps, or you're targeting organizations where $5K requires a procurement process.

**How to fix it:** For deals under $10K, the entire sales cycle should be under 30 days. If it's longer, you're either selling to the wrong persona, lacking urgency, or making the buying process too complex. Simplify: fewer calls, simpler contracts, easy payment (credit card, not PO).

**Ask yourself:** "Could a motivated buyer purchase our product in under a week if they wanted to?"

## Metrics & Experimentation Mistakes

Bad data practices don't just waste time — they give you false confidence, which is worse than having no data at all.

### Changing your North Star metric every quarter

**What it looks like:** Q1: "We're focused on signups." Q2: "Actually, activation is what matters." Q3: "We're pivoting to NRR." Q4: "We're tracking weekly active teams now." Nobody knows what success looks like.

**Why it happens:** Each quarter's metric was chosen because the previous one looked bad. The North Star keeps moving because leadership doesn't want to stare at a number that's not improving. It's metric avoidance disguised as strategic flexibility.

**How to fix it:** Pick one North Star metric and commit for at least a year. It should be the single best proxy for customer value delivery. Everything else is a supporting metric. If your North Star isn't moving, fix the inputs — don't change the metric.

**Ask yourself:** "If someone asked any employee what our most important metric is, would they all give the same answer?"

### A/B testing with insufficient sample size

**What it looks like:** You A/B tested a new pricing page for 5 days with 200 visitors. Variant B won with a 12% lift. You ship it. Next month, performance is flat. "A/B testing doesn't work."

**Why it happens:** Impatience. The tool turned green, you wanted a win to report, so you called it. Nobody calculated the required sample size before starting the test.

**How to fix it:** Calculate required sample size before you start. For most B2B SaaS (low traffic), you need 1,000-5,000 visitors per variant for reliable results. If you can't get that volume in 2-4 weeks, you probably shouldn't be A/B testing that page — make a judgment call instead.

**Ask yourself:** "How many visitors did each variant get, and what was our minimum detectable effect?"

### Optimizing for activation when retention is the problem

**What it looks like:** You've spent 3 months improving onboarding. Activation went from 15% to 25%. Churn is still 8% monthly. You're filling the bucket faster, but the hole in the bottom is the same size.

**Why it happens:** Activation is more fun to work on. It's a contained project with a clear before/after. Retention is diffuse, slow, and requires understanding why people leave — which is often uncomfortable to hear.

**How to fix it:** Fix retention first, always. There is no growth hack that overcomes 8% monthly churn. Talk to churned customers (they'll be more honest than active ones). Find the pattern. Fix it. Then improve activation. Then acquisition. Bottom of funnel up.

**Ask yourself:** "If we perfectly activated every new user, would our net revenue still grow?"

### Not segmenting metrics by cohort, plan, or channel

**What it looks like:** Your blended churn rate is 5% monthly. You think that's your churn rate. In reality, your free-to-paid cohort churns at 12%, your sales-led cohort churns at 2%, and your organic cohort churns at 4%. The actions needed for each are completely different.

**Why it happens:** Blended metrics are simpler. Segmentation requires better tooling and more analytical rigor. Most early-stage dashboards don't segment because nobody set it up.

**How to fix it:** Segment everything by cohort (when they signed up), plan (what they're paying), and channel (how they found you). At minimum. The blended number hides what's actually happening. You can't fix a problem you can't see.

**Ask yourself:** "Do we know which acquisition channel produces our highest-LTV customers?"

### Celebrating experiments that "won" with p=0.15

**What it looks like:** "Our new checkout flow increased conversions by 8%! (p=0.15, but it's directionally positive!)" You ship it. It was noise.

**Why it happens:** Confirmation bias. You wanted the experiment to win. The tool showed a green arrow. p=0.15 means there's a 15% chance the result is random — that's a coin flip with extra steps.

**How to fix it:** Set your significance threshold before the experiment starts (p<0.05 is standard). If the result doesn't meet it, the experiment is inconclusive — not "directionally positive." Inconclusive means you didn't learn anything and need more data or a bigger change.

**Ask yourself:** "If this experiment showed a negative result with the same p-value, would we still ship it?"

## Partnership Pitfalls

Partnerships are the "we should do a podcast" of B2B strategy. Everyone talks about them, few execute them, and the ones that get signed rarely produce revenue.

### Signing partnership agreements with no activation plan

**What it looks like:** You spent 3 months negotiating a partnership agreement with a larger company. You signed it. It sits in a Google Drive folder. Neither side has done anything in 6 months.

**Why it happens:** The partnership itself felt like the achievement. The signing was the milestone. Nobody planned what happens after the ink dries because the hard part was supposed to be getting the deal done.

**How to fix it:** Before signing, both sides should agree on a 90-day activation plan with specific deliverables, owners, and deadlines. First co-marketing campaign within 30 days. First co-sold deal within 90 days. If you can't agree on an activation plan, the partnership isn't real.

**Ask yourself:** "What specific revenue-generating activity will both sides do in the first 30 days?"

### Revenue share deals where neither side sells

**What it looks like:** You have a revenue share agreement: they get 20% of any deal they refer. You get 15% of any deal you refer. Total referrals exchanged in 12 months: zero.

**Why it happens:** Revenue share sounds like a free distribution channel. "They'll sell for us!" They won't. Their sales team has their own quota, their own products, and zero incentive to learn yours.

**How to fix it:** Don't do revenue share unless one side is already selling the other's product organically. Revenue share formalizes existing behavior — it doesn't create new behavior. If there's no organic referral flow already happening, a rev share agreement won't create one.

**Ask yourself:** "Has this partner referred a customer to us even once without a formal agreement?"

### Integration partnerships without co-marketing commitment

**What it looks like:** You built a Slack integration. You're listed in the Slack App Directory. Your listing has 12 installs. You expected Slack to drive distribution. They have 2,000 apps in the directory and don't know you exist.

**Why it happens:** "If we integrate with [big platform], their users will find us." They won't. Integration directories are graveyards. You're betting on organic discovery in a marketplace with thousands of competitors.

**How to fix it:** The integration is a feature, not a distribution channel. You still need to market it. Negotiate co-marketing commitment as part of the integration partnership — joint blog post, inclusion in a newsletter, feature in their marketplace, co-hosted webinar. If they won't co-market, the integration is still worth building, but don't count on it for growth.

**Ask yourself:** "Beyond listing us in a directory, what will this partner actually do to drive adoption of the integration?"

### Expecting partners to sell for you

**What it looks like:** "Once we sign this partnership with Acme Corp, their 500-person sales team will start recommending us." Spoiler: they won't.

**Why it happens:** Founders overestimate how much a larger partner cares about them. You're one of 50 "strategic partners." Their sales team has never heard of you and has no incentive to bring you up.

**How to fix it:** Make it stupid easy for partner sales reps to recommend you. One-page cheat sheet. Clear "when to recommend" triggers. Spiffs or incentives. And get embedded in their sales enablement — not just their partner page. Even then, expect 5% of their reps to engage, not 100%.

**Ask yourself:** "Can a partner sales rep explain what we do and when to recommend us in under 30 seconds?"

### The "we'll figure out the GTM later" partnership

**What it looks like:** "Let's sign the partnership and figure out the go-to-market motion later." Six months later, you still haven't figured it out.

**Why it happens:** GTM planning is hard. It requires aligning incentives, creating materials, training teams, and coordinating calendars. It's easier to sign the agreement and celebrate the "strategic win."

**How to fix it:** No GTM plan = no partnership. Period. Before signing, answer: Who sells what? To whom? How are leads routed? Who creates the materials? What's the joint value proposition? If you can't answer these questions, you don't have a partnership — you have a press release.

**Ask yourself:** "If a prospect asks 'why should I buy this through your partner?', do we have a compelling answer?"

## Hiring Mistakes

Bad hires in growth roles don't just cost salary — they cost 6-12 months of misdirection, a team that optimizes for the wrong things, and the opportunity cost of what a good hire would have done.

### Hiring a VP of Sales before you have a repeatable sales process

**What it looks like:** You hire a VP of Sales at $250K OTE to "build the sales machine." They spend 6 months building a CRM, a sales deck, a comp plan, and an org chart. Revenue doesn't move. They blame the product. You blame them. Everyone's right.

**Why it happens:** Founders hate selling and want to delegate it as fast as possible. A VP of Sales sounds like the answer. But a VP of Sales optimizes and scales a process — they don't create one from scratch. That's the founder's job.

**How to fix it:** Founders sell until they've closed 20-30 deals personally and can document the repeatable process. Then hire a sales manager to run the process. Then hire a VP to scale the team. Skipping steps doesn't save time — it wastes it.

**Ask yourself:** "Can I write down our sales process, including discovery questions, objection handling, and close techniques, from memory?"

### Hiring a growth marketer and expecting them to do everything

**What it looks like:** Job post: "Growth Marketer — own our entire go-to-market strategy including content, paid, SEO, product analytics, sales enablement, community, partnerships, and events." Salary: $85K. You're looking for a unicorn that doesn't exist.

**Why it happens:** Small teams need people to wear multiple hats. But "growth" has become a catch-all for "everything marketing and some sales and maybe product too." No single person can do content, paid, SEO, analytics, and partnerships well.

**How to fix it:** Pick the one channel that matters most right now. Hire for that. A great content marketer won't be a great paid acquisition specialist. A great product growth person won't be a great event marketer. Hire for the specific skill you need, not for a job title that means everything.

**Ask yourself:** "If this person could only do one thing for us, what would it be?"

### Hiring from big companies for a 10-person startup

**What it looks like:** You hired a "Head of Growth" from Google. They're used to managing $10M budgets and teams of 15. Your budget is $5K/month and they need to write their own copy. They're paralyzed.

**Why it happens:** Big-company pedigree is impressive and feels like a safe bet. "If they could do it at Google, they can do it here." Wrong. Big-company operators and startup builders are fundamentally different skill sets.

**How to fix it:** Hire people who've worked at companies one stage ahead of yours, not three stages ahead. If you're at $1M ARR, hire from companies that recently went from $1M to $5M, not from companies at $500M. They'll know your problems, your tools, and your constraints.

**Ask yourself:** "Has this person ever had to do this job with no budget, no team, and no brand recognition?"

### "We need a growth hacker"

**What it looks like:** "We need someone who can 10x our growth with creative hacks!" What you actually need is someone to set up proper analytics, fix your onboarding flow, and write landing pages that convert.

**Why it happens:** "Growth hacking" sounds exciting. It implies there's a shortcut — some clever trick that will unlock exponential growth. There isn't. Growth is mostly boring, systematic work.

**How to fix it:** Stop using the term "growth hacker." Define the specific problems you need solved. If you need better conversion rates, hire a product marketer. If you need more traffic, hire a content marketer or SEO specialist. If you need better retention, hire a product manager. "Growth hacker" is a title that attracts people who want to run experiments but not do the unglamorous work.

**Ask yourself:** "If we listed the actual daily tasks this person would do, would anyone call it 'growth hacking'?"
